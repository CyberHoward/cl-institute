**Interview Transcript — ASADA Playas de Nosara**
**Subject: Carta de Agua Process**
**Interviewer: [Research Team]**
**Administrator: Don Carlos Mora, ASADA Administrator**

---

**Interviewer:** Thanks for making time for us today, Don Carlos. Can you start by just telling us in your own words — what is a Carta de Agua, and why does someone need one?

**Administrator:** Of course. The Carta de Agua — sometimes we call it the *constancia de disponibilidad hídrica* — is basically our official letter saying whether we can supply water to a specific property or project. The municipality requires it before they'll issue a construction permit. So if someone wants to build a house, a hotel, expand a business — they need to come to us first. We have to tell them: yes, we have the capacity, or no, we don't. It sounds simple, but it's actually one of the most sensitive things we do here because it affects how the whole community grows.

---

**Interviewer:** And how does the process start? How does someone actually submit a request?

**Administrator:** They can come to us in a few ways. The most common is still in person, right here at the office. We also receive requests by email and through WhatsApp — that's very common here in Nosara, as you can imagine. People send us a message on WhatsApp saying, "I need a carta for my land." And that's fine as a way to get the ball rolling, but I always explain: WhatsApp is for starting the process and sending documents. The actual decision doesn't happen on WhatsApp. It has to be formal.

---

**Interviewer:** What happens the moment a request comes in?

**Administrator:** The first thing we do — the very first thing — is assign a Case ID and send the person a receipt. That's non-negotiable. It doesn't matter if they came in person or sent a WhatsApp message at eight o'clock at night. Before anything else happens, there is a case with a number and a timestamp, and they get confirmation that we received it. That way there are no "invisible" requests. It protects us and it protects the applicant.

---

**Interviewer:** And after that, what does the applicant need to provide?

**Administrator:** At minimum, they need the request form — we have a standard form — and the *plano catastrado*, the cadastral plan for the property. That's the legal parcel reference. Without that, we can't even verify what property we're talking about. If the person is already one of our *abonados* — an existing water subscriber — we also check that their payments are current. We can't process a new request if someone owes us for the service they already have.

---

**Interviewer:** What if they're missing something when they first come in?

**Administrator:** Very common. We go through a completeness checklist — we look at everything they've submitted — and if something is missing, we send them a notice that says exactly what's still needed and how to submit it. We try to be very specific. Not just "you're missing documents," but "you need to provide the cadastral plan and proof of payment." The case stays open but it doesn't advance until we have the minimum evidence. We call this the pending documents stage.

---

**Interviewer:** How do they receive that notice?

**Administrator:** Depends on how they came to us. If they're in front of me, I tell them directly and give them the checklist on paper. If they contacted us by WhatsApp, I send it there. If they came by email, they get an email. We try to meet people where they are. But the formal case record is always here in our system regardless of the channel.

---

**Interviewer:** Once the documents are complete, what happens next?

**Administrator:** Then we do a triage. We classify the case — is it a simple low-impact residential request? Is it a commercial project? Is it a hotel or a large development? That classification matters a lot because it determines how much scrutiny the case gets and which path it follows. We also check at that point whether there's a scarcity situation. If we're in the dry season and we have an active hold because the source is stressed, the case goes into a hold state. The applicant gets a written notice explaining why and when we expect to review the situation. We're very careful not to just put things in a drawer without explanation.

---

**Interviewer:** Can you say more about how you communicate with people during the process? Status updates, that kind of thing?

**Administrator:** Yes, this is something I feel strongly about. A lot of frustration — and frankly, a lot of the pressure people put on us — comes from not knowing what's happening. "Why is it taking so long? Did you lose my papers?" So we try to send status updates at key moments. When their documents are complete and we start the review, they hear from us. If we need to schedule an inspection, we let them know. If the case is scheduled for the board meeting, we tell them. People can also contact us directly to ask — the WhatsApp or email are open for questions. But the answers always come from the official case record.

---

**Interviewer:** You mentioned inspections. When does an inspection happen?

**Administrator:** Not always. For a straightforward residential request on a well-connected street, if our technical person can verify capacity from what we already know about the network and the source, we may not need to go out to the site. But for larger projects, for anything with high impact on the system — hotels, multi-unit developments — almost always we need an inspection. The technical operator goes out, checks the specific location, takes notes, sometimes photographs. That report becomes part of the case file and it goes to the board.

---

**Interviewer:** Tell us about the board. How does the Junta Directiva fit in?

**Administrator:** The board makes the final decision. I can do a lot — I receive the request, I check completeness, I coordinate the technical review — but I cannot approve or deny a carta on my own. That authority belongs to the Junta. What I prepare for them is a packet: the request, the evidence, the technical verification note, any inspection report, and a summary of what the technical person recommends. The board reviews it, discusses it if needed, and makes the decision: approve, deny, conditional approval, or defer if they want more information.

---

**Interviewer:** What does a conditional approval look like?

**Administrator:** A conditional approval means: yes, we can provide water, but only under certain specific conditions. For example, maybe the project has to install its own storage tank first, or they can only connect during certain phases of construction. Those conditions have to be written explicitly. That's very important to me — we don't make informal promises. "We'll figure it out when you're ready to connect" — that's not acceptable. If there's a condition, it's in writing in the letter and it's logged in the case.

---

**Interviewer:** And how is the decision delivered to the applicant?

**Administrator:** We generate the official letter, the President or Secretary signs it — or both, depending on the case — and we deliver it. For a denial or a conditional approval, the letter also explains the reasons and tells the person how they can ask questions or appeal. We send it the same way they've been communicating with us. Some people prefer to come pick it up in person. Others want it by email. Developers sometimes need us to send a copy directly to the municipality as well.

---

**Interviewer:** What if someone disagrees with the decision?

**Administrator:** They have the right to contest it. We make sure that's always clear in the decision letter — there's an explanation of what the basis for the decision was, and a description of how to submit an appeal. If someone submits an appeal, we give them a receipt right away, same as the initial intake. The appeal goes back to the board for review. They look at the grounds, they review the evidence, and they issue a decision. Every step of that is logged.

---

**Interviewer:** How long does the whole process typically take from start to finish?

**Administrator:** For a simple, complete residential case with no complications, realistically somewhere between five and fifteen business days. That assumes they give us everything upfront. The big variable is the board schedule — they meet regularly, but if a case comes in right after a meeting, it may have to wait for the next one. For high-impact cases that require inspection and more extensive technical review, it can take longer. We try to be honest about that upfront rather than give people false expectations.

---

**Interviewer:** Last question: what would you say is the biggest challenge in this whole process?

**Administrator:** Pressure. Development pressure. This is a beautiful community, and a lot of people want to build here. We have developers, we have tourism investors, we have everyone telling us that their project is important, that it's urgent. And I understand. But my obligation is to the people who already live here and depend on this water. If I approve too many cartas and the system can't handle it, everyone suffers during the dry season. So the process has to hold. The case file, the technical verification, the board decision — that's not bureaucracy for its own sake. It's what protects us from making commitments we can't keep.
